Classical AI method families are named for a mechanism: the heuristic in heuristic search, the constraints in constraint propagation, the swarm in particle swarm optimisation, the learning in reinforcement learning. Textbooks credit it with the family's performance. We ask, across four families, whether the eponym does the work. We build four problems in one setting (urban mobility in Dhaka), implement each family from scratch, and ablate them at matched compute budgets, scoring each on its customary metric and on one that charges every resource used.

No standard attribution survives intact, and in three families the customary metric conceals the failure. (i) Informed search: three "different" routing heuristics are positive scalar multiples of one function, so greedy best-first is provably invariant to the choice and A* over them is a one-parameter weight sweep. Nodes-expanded ranks A* above uniform-cost search by 2.15x; wall clock ranks it below by 6.18x, because 91.6% of A*'s runtime is a one-off O(|E|) calibration the metric prices at zero. The fastest exact method we measure is uninformed. (ii) Constraint satisfaction: ordering and propagation cut node counts as folklore predicts, and run 12-14x slower. (iii) Population-based search: thirty non-communicating particles are indistinguishable from random search (p = 0.22), one particle is worse (p < 1e-4); the social term alone buys 7.6 minutes of expected wait. The population is not the mechanism; the communication is. (iv) Reinforcement learning: on identical data, planning on an estimated model beats a tuned model-free learner (1.74% vs 2.72% regret), and planning with arrival rates wrong tenfold still wins.

In each family the operative component is one whose cost is paid once and reused many times, while the eponym's cost is paid per unit of work -- and each subfield's metric declines to charge for reuse.
